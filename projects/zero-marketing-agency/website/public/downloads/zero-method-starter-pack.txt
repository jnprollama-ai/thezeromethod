## EMAIL & COMMUNICATION (5 prompts)

### Prompt #1: Professional Email Polisher
**Use Case:** Turn rough notes into professional emails

```
I need to send a professional email about: [topic]

Here's what I want to communicate:
[your rough notes or bullet points]

Context:
- Recipient: [who you're emailing]
- Relationship: [formal / casual / client]
- Goal: [what you want them to do]
- Urgency: [low / medium / high]

Please write a clear, professional email that:
1. Has an appropriate subject line
2. Gets straight to the point
3. Includes a clear call-to-action
4. Matches the tone to the relationship
5. Is no longer than necessary
```

---

### Prompt #2: Difficult Conversation Starter
**Use Case:** Say no, push back, or address issues professionally

```
I need to [say no to / push back on / address an issue with]:
[situation]

Key points:
- What's happening: [brief description]
- Why I can't agree: [your reason]
- What I can offer instead: [alternative, if any]
- Relationship importance: [high / medium / low]

Write a response that is:
1. Clear and direct (no beating around the bush)
2. Professional but firm
3. Brief (2-3 paragraphs max)
4. Leaves the door open for future collaboration
5. Doesn't over-explain or apologize excessively
```

---

### Prompt #3: Follow-Up That Gets a Response
**Use Case:** Following up without being pushy

```
I sent [what you sent] to [who] on [date] and haven't heard back.

Original context:
[brief reminder of what you sent]

Their likely situation: [busy / forgot / not interested / other]

Write a follow-up email that:
1. Acknowledges they're busy
2. Reminds them briefly what you sent
3. Makes it easy to respond (yes/no or short answer)
4. Suggests a specific next step
5. Sounds helpful, not desperate

Subject line options: [include 2-3 subject line options]
```

---

### Prompt #4: Meeting Request That Actually Works
**Use Case:** Get meetings with busy people

```
I want to request a meeting with [person/role] about [topic].

Value for them:
[what they'll get from this meeting]

Time needed: [15 min / 30 min / 1 hour]

Draft a meeting request that:
1. Leads with value for THEM (not you)
2. Shows you've done your research
3. Suggests specific times (3 options)
4. Makes it easy to say yes
5. Includes an "out" if they're not interested

Keep it under 150 words.
```

---

### Prompt #5: Introduction Email (Making Connections)
**Use Case:** Introduce two people who should meet

```
I want to introduce [Person A] to [Person B] because [reason].

Person A:
- Who they are: [brief]
- Why they're relevant: [connection point]

Person B:
- Who they are: [brief]
- Why they're relevant: [connection point]

Value of the connection: [what could come from this]

Write a double-opt-in introduction:
1. Separate paragraphs for each person
2. Why each should care about the other
3. Clear next step (suggest they connect directly)
4. BCC both (explain this in the email)
```

## CONTENT CREATION (5 prompts)

### Prompt #11: Blog Post from Brain Dump
**Use Case:** Turn ideas into structured articles

```
I want to write about: [topic]

My rough ideas:
[bullet points, notes, stream of consciousness]

Target audience: [who should read this]

Goal: [educate / persuade / entertain / drive action]

Desired length: [short (500w) / medium (1000w) / long (2000w)]

Turn this into:
1. 5 headline options (different angles)
2. Introduction hook (first 100 words)
3. Article outline (H2s and H3s)
4. Key points to cover under each section
5. Call-to-action for the end
6. 3 SEO keywords to include

Don't write the full article - just the framework.
```

### Prompt #13: Case Study Structure
**Use Case:** Turn client success into marketing content


## PRODUCTIVITY & PLANNING (5 prompts)

### Prompt #16: Daily Prioritizer
**Use Case:** Plan your day in 2 minutes

```
Here's what I need to do today:
[dump everything on your mind]

Energy level: [high / medium / low]

Deadlines: [what must happen today]

Prioritize this:
1. Must do today (max 3 items)
2. Should do today (if time allows)
3. Could do today (if everything else done)
4. Move to tomorrow (everything else)

For each "must do": Suggest a time block
For everything else: Suggest when to revisit
```

### Prompt #17: Decision Framework
**Use Case:** Make hard decisions faster

```
Decision: [what you're deciding]

Option A:
- Pros: [list]
- Cons: [list]

Option B:
- Pros: [list]
- Cons: [list]

Other options: [if any]

Constraints: [budget / time / resources]

Help me decide:
1. Frame the decision clearly
2. Identify the 2-3 most important factors
3. Compare options against those factors
4. Recommend the best choice with reasoning
5. Flag any risks or downsides
6. Suggest next steps

Don't just list pros/cons - actually recommend something.
```

## RESEARCH & LEARNING (5 prompts)

### Prompt #21: Topic Explainer
**Use Case:** Learn complex topics fast

```
Explain: [topic/concept]

My current understanding: [beginner / intermediate / advanced]

Why I need to know this: [context]

Give me:
1. One-sentence definition (ELI5 style)
2. Why it matters (the "so what")
3. Key concepts (3-5 terms with brief definitions)
4. Real-world examples (2-3)
5. Common misconceptions
6. Where to learn more (3 specific resources)

No jargon unless explained. Use analogies.
```

```
1. Core thesis (main argument in 2 sentences)

3. 2 practical applications to my work
4. 1 counter-argument or critique
5. Who should read this (and who shouldn't)
6. If I only read one chapter: which one and why
```

## Customization Guide

### Brackets Explained:
- **[Name]** = Recipient's name
- **[Specific value]** = Details unique to your situation
- **[Optional]** = Can remove if not applicable

### Tone Adjustments:

**More formal:**
- Use full sentences
- Avoid contractions
- Include titles

**More casual:**
- Shorter sentences
- Contractions okay
- Can remove formal sign-offs

### Pro Tips:

1. **Subject lines:** Specific > clever. "Q about your pricing page" beats "Quick question"
2. **Length:** Shorter = better. Aim for 100 words or less
3. **One ask:** Each email should have ONE clear purpose
4. **Easy out:** Always give them permission to say no
5. **Proofread:** Grammar mistakes kill credibility
